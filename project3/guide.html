How To Plan Your First Storytime!

Use this information to plan an awesome Storytime at your library!

  • Consider Your Audience

    When planning your first Storytimes, it is important to think about your audience. Is this Storytime aimed at babies who are still learning how to walk and talk? Or is it for older children who are getting ready to enter kindergarten? It is important to think about the developmental stage of your audience and their capabilities as you plan!

  • Think About Goals

    Every Storytime should serve a purpose. Most libraries center their Storytimes around the Every Child Ready to Read (ECRR) initiative. This initiative is centered around five core practices:

    • Talking
    • Reading
    • Singing
    • Writing
    • Playing

    By incorporating one or more of these practices into your Storytime, you are helping participants to develop important early literacy and pre-reading skills. You can also set personal goals, such as encouraging movement or focusing on developing motor skills with crafts and fingerplays.

  • Choose a Book (or two!)

    Pick out a book for your audience. Board books and simple picture books are great for younger kids, while older kids may enjoy something more interactive. Classic booksm like "Brown Bear, Brown Bear What Do You See" by Bill Martin Jr. and Eric Carlem generally do well for a variety of audiences and caregivers. In the end, pick a book you like and feel comfortable reading. Read it once or twice out loud before your Storytime for practice if you're nervous! Most Storytimes only have time for one book, but it's always helpful to have a back up!

  • Pick a Theme

    Having a theme for your Storytime can be helpful for some librarians. You can theme your Storytime around your book, or you can theme it around an upcoming event. You can even theme it around a color, a letter, an animal, or whatever you'd like! If you find yourself struggling with a theme, feel free to just pick activities you like. Attendees will have fun no matter what you plan!

  • Choose Activities

    Storytimes aren't just about books. They're also about moving, singing, and having fun! Try to find fun songs and rhymes that get children interacting. There are many blogs from fellow librarians with wonderful recommendations for songs, rhymes, movement activities, and fingerplays. Recorded songs are a great option as well. Explore, practice, and find ones that work for you!

  • Consider Adding Manipulatives

    Many Storytimes incorporate maniplulatives for children to engage with during the Storytime. Puppets, shaker eggs, scarfs, bells, and props are wonderful options that can be purchased online! There are also many tutorials for creating felt characters and objects to compliment your story or activities.

  • Make Your Schedule

    Storytimes are generally about 30 minutes, otherwise children can lose interest. Creating a schedule can help you stay on track, but don't feel pressured to follow it exactly! Feel free to change and adjust your schedule depending on your audience. If they seem fussy that day, it is okay to end a bit early or transition to a new activity. The following is an example schedule:

    1. Hello song
    2. Movement song
    3. Book
    4. Fingerplay rhyme
    5. Introduce manipulative
    6. Rhyme using manipulative
    7. Song using manipulative
    8. Collect manipulative
    9. Goodbye song

  • Have Fun!

    Storytimes should be fun for you as well! Experiment and see what works best for you and your style! Don't be afraid of mistakes. Your audience will never know if you messed up and they're just to happy to be reading with you!
